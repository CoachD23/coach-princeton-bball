Princeton Offense for High School Basketball

Coach Lee DeForest | February 3, 2025 8 min read

Why High School Coaches Choose the Princeton Offense

The Princeton Offense levels the playing field. High school coaches who run it consistently describe the same experience: their teams compete and beat opponents with significantly more athletic talent.

The reason is simple. The Princeton Offense puts decision-making at the center of the game — and good decisions beat athleticism more often than coaches expect.

Matching the System to Your Roster

The Princeton Offense works with almost any roster. You don't need elite athletes, elite shooters, or a dominant post player. You need players who can pass, cut, and read.

The key personnel requirement is a point guard who can make decisions under pressure and a team that buys into ball movement. Everything else is teachable.

Installation for High School Teams

Start with the core read: overplayed means backdoor. This single concept, drilled in the first week, establishes the foundation for everything that follows.

Week 1: Spacing and the basic cut. Week 2: The Chin set entry. Week 3: Counters and reads. Week 4: Full offense with competitive drills. This four-week framework gets most teams functional with the system.

Managing Player Buy-In

High school players accustomed to isolation basketball may resist ball movement initially. Show them the data: teams that average more assists per game outscore opponents at a significantly higher rate.

Culture change takes time. Celebrate every extra pass, every back-cut, every unselfish play — and the system will take hold.

Common Mistakes at the High School Level

The most common mistake is running the offense too fast. The Princeton Offense requires patience. Coaches who allow players to rush and force shots before the defense is broken lose the system's primary advantage.

The second mistake is skipping the read teaching. Players who run the movements without understanding the reads will be lost the moment a defense adjusts.
